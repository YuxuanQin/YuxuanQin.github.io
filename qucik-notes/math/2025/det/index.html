The definition of determinat

Definition

For vector space VV with base field 𝕜\mathbb{k} and a fixed basis (ei)(e_i), we use Altn(V){\mathrm{Alt}_n(V)} to denote its all alternating n-froms. It’s a well known fact that this is also a vector space with dimension 11.

For any endomorphism TT on VV, i.e., a matrix, we define a endomorphism of Altn(V){\mathrm{Alt}_n(V)} as α↦T*α\alpha \mapsto T^*\alpha, the latter applied to a vector (vi)(v_i) in VV as T*α(vi)=α(Tvi)T^*\alpha(v_i) = \alpha(Tv_i).

We thus obtain a endomorphism T*(−)T^*(-) on Altn(V){\mathrm{Alt}_n(V)}, since Altn(V)≅𝕜{\mathrm{Alt}_n(V)} \cong \mathbb{k}, T*T^* must be identified with a scalar multiplication c⋅−c \cdot -, and the constant c∈𝕜c \in \mathbb{k} is unique.

Finally, we define detT\mathrm{det}\ T as the unique constant cc in 𝕜\mathbb{k}.

Some quick facts

  • The determinat of identity matrix is what you want: det(id)=1\mathrm{det} (\mathrm{id}) = 1 by definition.
  • Determinat plays well with multiplication: det(AB)=detA⋅detB\mathrm{det} (A B) = \mathrm{det} A \cdot \mathrm{det} B for all morphisms AA and BB. Again this is a immediately result by definition.
  • Definition plays well with scalar: det(cA)=cndetA\mathrm{det}(c A) = c^n \mathrm{det} A, by definition.
